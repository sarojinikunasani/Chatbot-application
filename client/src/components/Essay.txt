Sweden’s energy system, as explained in the article, shows how planning and efficiency can really make a difference. One thing that stands out is their use of clean energy: they rely heavily on hydropower, wind, and nuclear energy. This helps reduce pollution and makes their system more sustainable. They also focus a lot on efficiency; for example, district heating systems help avoid energy waste. Another important point is their long-term planning—clear goals make it easier for both industries and people to follow the same direction.

In comparison, India still depends a lot on coal-based energy, especially in fast-growing areas. Although renewable energy is improving (like solar panels and wind farms), demand is increasing quickly. In cities like Bengaluru, power issues still happen sometimes-this shows there is a gap between supply and demand. Policies are present, but they are not always consistent; this creates challenges for growth.

If I were to advise the government, I would suggest focusing more on efficiency rather than only increasing production. Small changes can help a lot: better electricity management, reduced wastage, and improved infrastructure. For example, smart grids can help track and control energy usage more effectively. Also, awareness among people is very important! Many people still don’t think about saving electricity in daily life. Simple habits, like turning off unused devices, can make a difference.

As the article suggests, “consistent planning leads to better results” (Sweden.se, n.d.). India can learn from this approach, but it should adapt it based on local needs. For instance, rural areas can benefit from small solar systems (low-cost and easy to maintain). Overall, Sweden’s model is efficient and well-managed; India is improving but still has a long way to go. With better planning, awareness, and strong policies, India can build a more efficient energy system—this is definitely possible!
